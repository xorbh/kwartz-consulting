It's easy to fall into the pattern of rewarding activity. Meetings fill calendars. Emails pile up. People stay late. And it feels like progress, because at least something is happening.

Cal Newport nailed this when he coined **pseudo-productivity**: using visible activity as a stand-in for actual output. It made sense on a factory floor where you could count widgets. Knowledge work never figured out its own version of that, so we fell back on the only thing we could see: busyness.

Then AI agents showed up. And they're changing the equation.

## Agents can't fake it

You can't tell an agent to "be a team player." You can't reward it for staying late or promote it for showing up to meetings. An agent needs exactly one thing: a clear, measurable outcome to work toward. Give it that, it performs. Don't, and it flails. Or worse, it optimizes for the wrong thing entirely.

This turns out to be a useful mirror. Where goals are vague or accountability is fuzzy, agents surface that quickly, because they can't fill in the gaps with effort the way people naturally do.

Gartner predicts over 40% of agentic AI projects will get cancelled by 2027, and in most cases the technology isn't the issue. The challenge is organizational: getting clear enough about desired outcomes to give agents something meaningful to work toward.

## We already have the data

Microsoft's Work Trend Index paints the picture:

- Only **12% of leaders** have full confidence their team is productive
- **87% of employees** say they are productive
- Workers get interrupted **275 times per day** during core hours
- **58 chats sent daily outside work hours**, up 15% year over year

That gap between "12% of leaders think we're productive" and "87% of workers say they are" is worth sitting with. It suggests that when productivity isn't clearly defined, people naturally default to their own interpretation, which tends to look like whatever they're already doing.

Compare that to organizations that actually tried measuring outcomes instead of hours. Best Buy's corporate team shifted to a results-only model and saw **35% higher productivity** with **90% less voluntary turnover**. JA Counter and Associates saw **94% higher net income**. Dynatronix got their on-time delivery to 90%, a 20 point improvement.

These results have been available for a while. Agents are now creating the momentum to take them seriously.

## Why this forces better leadership

When you sit down to deploy an agent on a real business process, you run into questions that are easy to defer in day-to-day management but hard to skip with an agent:

- What outcome does this process exist to produce?
- How do we know if it's working?
- What does done actually look like?

You can't hand-wave past these with an agent the way you can with a person. A person will figure out something to do. An agent will do exactly what you told it to, which means you'd better be clear about what you told it.

McKinsey's research on agentic organizations lands on this point hard: performance management has to move from tracking task completion to tracking whether people are orchestrating agents toward actual outcomes. The most fundamental shift they identify is that human roles stop being about executing activities and start being about owning end-to-end outcomes.

That shift applies to how we manage people too. Agents are just making the opportunity more visible.

## Judgment over blame

There's a related change happening around accountability. It's common for accountability systems to drift toward identifying blame rather than improving results. Not by design, but because that's the path of least resistance.

Agents don't fit that model. When an agent produces a bad result, "whose fault is it" is the wrong question. The right questions are: was the outcome defined well? Were the constraints reasonable? Did someone exercise good judgment in setting this up?

That's the real shift, from liability to judgment. And judgment is harder. It means leaders have to actually decide things. Define what matters. Own the frameworks their teams operate in rather than just policing whether people followed the process.

Organizations with genuine accountability practices (not blame cultures) are **50% more likely to hit their strategic goals**. The difference matters because blame teaches people to hide problems. Accountability teaches people to own outcomes. These produce very different organizations over time.

HBR recently laid out five types of judgment that matter now:

1. **Evaluative**: can you tell if a result is actually good?
2. **Contextual**: do you know when standard rules apply and when they don't?
3. **Tradeoff**: can you weigh competing priorities honestly?
4. **Anticipatory**: do you see second-order consequences before they land?
5. **Ownership**: will you take responsibility when things are uncertain?

None of these have anything to do with hours logged, emails sent, or meetings attended.

## What to do about it

If you're leading an organization through this transition, a few things matter:

**Define outcomes, not activities.** It's worth asking: can you articulate the specific outcome a role or process exists to produce? "Improve customer satisfaction" is a direction. "Cut resolution time from 4 hours to 90 minutes at 95% satisfaction" is an outcome. The exercise of getting that specific is valuable whether or not you're deploying an agent.

**Stop counting inputs.** Hours, tasks completed, emails sent. None of these tell you whether value got created. They just tell you people were busy. We have decades of data showing that busy and productive are different things.

**Build accountability into the system.** Design your processes so outcomes are visible and people can course-correct in real time. That's different from designing systems that exist to assign blame after the fact.

**Develop judgment deliberately.** AI is going to handle more of the routine work that used to train junior people. That's a problem, because those reps are how people build judgment. "Human in the loop" doesn't solve this on its own. You need real structures for growing the evaluative and contextual skills your organization will run on.

**Accept that this is harder.** Counting hours is straightforward. Defining outcomes takes clarity, courage, and discipline. But that's also where the leverage is.

## The bottom line

Gallup's data shows top-quartile engaged teams produce **21% higher profitability** and **20% higher sales** than bottom-quartile teams. And 70% of that engagement traces back to the manager. Leadership clarity has outsized impact. It always has.

AI agents didn't create the tension between activity and productivity. They just brought it into sharper focus. The organizations that do well from here will likely be the ones where leaders got disciplined about defining what actually matters.

The agents need your answer to that question. Your people have been waiting for it even longer.
